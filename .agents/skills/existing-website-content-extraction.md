# Agent Skill: Existing Website Content Extraction

Use this doc to analyze an existing client website and extract usable content for a homepage redesign, website content strategy, copy map, or wireframe planning task.

This is not a final copywriting task unless explicitly requested. The goal is to identify what useful information already exists on the client’s current website, organize it, flag what can be reused, and translate it into practical website planning inputs.

## Core Rules

Treat the existing website as supporting context, not absolute truth.

If an intake form is also provided, the intake form is the primary source of truth. The existing website should support, clarify, or fill gaps in the intake.

Do not invent unsupported claims, certifications, guarantees, review counts, pricing, awards, financing, emergency availability, years in business, warranties, service promises, or operational details.

Do not assume outdated website content is still accurate. Flag anything that should be confirmed before final copywriting.

Do not scrape or reuse third-party content that appears embedded from outside sources unless the client clearly owns it or it is already publicly presented as part of their site.

## Primary Objective

Extract and organize useful website content that can guide:

* Homepage structure
* Service hierarchy
* Copy direction
* CTA strategy
* Contact section content
* Footer content
* Trust messaging
* FAQ direction
* Service area copy
* Visual/image direction
* Testimonial usage
* Content gaps
* Copy guardrails

## Source Priority

Use this source confidence order:

1. Intake form — highest confidence
2. Existing client website — supporting context
3. Client-owned testimonials already shown on their website — usable with caution
4. Google Business Profile or third-party listings — reference only unless provided through approved access
5. Assumptions — allowed only when needed and clearly labeled

When sources conflict, flag the conflict instead of choosing silently.

Example:

* Intake says they do not offer emergency service.
* Website says “24/7 emergency repair.”
* Output should flag this as a conflict that must be confirmed.

## What to Extract

Review the current website and extract the following where available.

### Business Basics

Look for:

* Business name
* Logo or brand name styling
* Phone number
* Email address
* Address
* Hours
* Service model
* Contact methods
* Social links
* Google Business Profile link
* License numbers or legal footer details if shown

Use this for:

* Header
* Footer
* Contact page
* Local SEO basics
* Business info section
* Schema-ish business details

### Services

Look for:

* Main services
* Sub-services
* Service page titles
* Repeated services in nav/footer
* Services emphasized on the homepage
* Services hidden deeper in the site
* Services mentioned only once
* Services with dedicated pages
* Services that seem outdated or unclear

Use this for:

* Service cards
* Services overview
* Service page structure
* Homepage hierarchy
* Contact form dropdowns
* Priority service recommendations

### Service Area

Look for:

* Cities served
* Neighborhoods served
* Counties served
* “Near me” or local SEO copy
* Location pages
* Maps
* Address/service-area mismatch
* Areas repeated in footer or homepage

Use this for:

* Service area section
* Local copy
* Footer service area line
* Future location page ideas

### Lead Flow

Look for:

* Primary CTA wording
* Secondary CTA wording
* Phone-first vs form-first behavior
* Quote request form
* Booking form
* Emergency call path
* Contact page layout
* Thank-you page if visible
* Form fields
* Required fields
* Scheduling language
* Financing or estimate language if shown

Use this for:

* CTA labels
* Form recommendations
* Urgency path
* Thank-you message direction
* Follow-up automation ideas

### Trust Points

Look for:

* Licensed / insured claims
* Family-owned or locally owned claims
* Years in business
* Certifications
* Guarantees
* Warranty mentions
* Review count or rating claims
* Testimonials
* Brand/product certifications
* Team photos
* Before/after photos
* Professional process claims
* Safety/background-check claims
* Memberships or associations

Use this for:

* Trust bar
* Why choose us section
* About section
* Proof bullets
* Reassurance copy

Only reuse claims that are clearly stated. If a claim seems important but unsupported, flag it for confirmation.

### Customer Questions / Objections

Look for:

* FAQ pages
* FAQ accordions
* Service page explanations
* Pricing questions
* Process explanations
* Repair vs replacement guidance
* Timing/scheduling expectations
* Maintenance plan explanations
* Emergency service explanations
* Warranty/guarantee explanations
* Financing explanations

Use this for:

* FAQ direction
* Objection-handling sections
* Service page helper copy
* Contact form helper text

### Assets / Visual Direction

Look for:

* Real project photos
* Team photos
* Trucks/vans
* Location photos
* Product/system photos
* Before/after images
* Icons
* Badges
* Promo graphics
* Seasonal banners
* Brand colors
* Type style
* General visual tone

Use this for:

* Homepage image direction
* Hero image recommendation
* Service card image/icon direction
* About section visuals
* Trust/proof visuals
* Promo placement
* Landing page ideas

Do not assume photos are client-owned if they appear to be generic stock images. Flag stock-looking imagery.

### Offers / Promotions

Look for:

* Seasonal offers
* Coupons
* Maintenance plans
* Free estimate language
* Financing language
* Discounts
* New customer offers
* Bundles
* Membership plans

Use this for:

* Promo banners
* CTA support copy
* Landing page ideas
* Homepage callouts

Do not reuse an offer unless it appears current or the client confirms it.

### Testimonials

Look for testimonials already displayed on the client’s website.

Extract:

* Testimonial text
* Customer name or initials if shown
* Location if shown
* Star rating if shown
* Service mentioned if shown
* Source if shown

Use testimonials carefully:

* For mockups or concept work, testimonials already shown on the client website may be used as source material.
* For a live client site, testimonials should be client-approved.
* Do not invent customer names, ratings, review counts, or review text.
* Do not edit testimonials in a way that changes meaning.
* Do not imply Google review content is owned by the client unless it is displayed through an approved method or provided by the client.
* If testimonials are from Google, Yelp, Facebook, or another third-party platform, flag them as requiring approval/attribution before live use.

## Analysis Method

Read the website once for facts.

Read it again for conversion implications.

For each major content area, determine:

1. What the website explicitly says.
2. Where the information appears.
3. Whether the information seems current.
4. Whether the information supports homepage strategy.
5. Whether the information supports service hierarchy.
6. Whether the information supports trust or proof.
7. Whether the information supports lead capture.
8. Whether anything conflicts with the intake.
9. Whether anything should be confirmed before final copywriting.

## Prioritization Rule

Do not treat every piece of extracted content as equally important.

Prioritize content that helps a visitor:

* Understand what the business does
* Know whether they are in the service area
* Trust the business
* Choose the right service
* Contact the business quickly
* Feel reassured about the process

When completeness conflicts with clarity, prioritize the information most likely to support conversion.

## Required Output Format

Use the structure below.

### Existing Website Content Summary

Briefly summarize what useful content the current site provides and how strong it is as source material.

Mention whether the site appears to have:

* Clear services
* Clear contact info
* Clear service area
* Usable trust proof
* Usable testimonials
* Useful FAQs
* Useful images/assets
* Missing or unclear content

### Extracted Business Facts

List confirmed facts found on the website.

Organize into:

* Business name
* Contact info
* Hours
* Address / service location
* Service area
* Main services
* Offers/promos
* Trust claims
* Testimonials
* Other useful details

### Service Hierarchy Notes

Identify:

* Main services
* Services emphasized most
* Services with dedicated pages
* Services that may deserve homepage cards
* Services that may be lower priority
* Services that need confirmation

Explain how the current website suggests the service hierarchy should work.

### Usable Copy / Messaging Inputs

Extract useful messaging patterns, phrases, or themes.

Do not write final copy unless asked.

Organize into:

* Possible hero direction
* Service messaging themes
* Trust messaging themes
* About/company themes
* CTA direction
* FAQ/objection themes

### Homepage / Wireframe Implications

Explain how the extracted content should influence homepage structure.

Recommend:

* What should appear high on the page
* What proof should appear early
* Which services should be featured
* Where CTAs should repeat
* Where service area content should appear
* Whether testimonials should be included
* Whether a promo/offer section is useful
* What content can appear lower on the page

### Lead Capture Notes

Extract and recommend:

* Current CTA wording
* Better CTA wording if needed
* Phone vs form emphasis
* Suggested form fields based on current site
* Urgent lead path if applicable
* Thank-you page direction
* Owner/customer notification logic if useful

Do not overcomplicate lead flow unless the website or intake justifies it.

### Trust / Proof Notes

List usable trust signals from the website.

Organize into:

* Trust bar candidates
* Why choose us candidates
* About section themes
* Testimonial candidates
* Visual proof candidates
* Claims needing confirmation

Avoid vague claims like “best service” unless directly supported.

### FAQ / Objection Notes

List existing or implied FAQ topics based on the website.

Focus on questions around:

* Pricing
* Timing
* Scheduling
* Service process
* Repair vs replacement
* Emergency availability
* Service area
* Maintenance
* Estimates
* Warranties or guarantees
* Financing

Do not write full FAQ answers unless asked.

### Visual / Asset Notes

Summarize available visual material and how it could be used.

Include:

* Real photos
* Stock-looking photos
* Team photos
* Project photos
* Logo/brand assets
* Icons/badges
* Promo graphics
* Image gaps

Flag assets that may need replacement.

### Conflicts / Risks / Guardrails

List anything that should not be used without confirmation.

Include:

* Outdated offers
* Conflicting service claims
* Unsupported trust claims
* Third-party review content
* Emergency service claims
* Warranty/guarantee claims
* Pricing claims
* Financing claims
* Review count/rating claims
* Services that may no longer be offered
* Any claim that seems legally or operationally risky

### Content Gaps / Follow-Up Questions

List missing information that would help before final copywriting.

Separate:

* Must confirm before publishing
* Nice to have
* Reasonable assumptions

Do not let gaps stop the analysis. Make the best practical recommendation with the available website content.

## Tone and Output Quality

Keep the output practical, skimmable, and website-focused.

Write for a designer, strategist, or website builder who needs to turn an existing site into a better homepage or content plan.

Prefer clear recommendations over exhaustive commentary.

Be specific, grounded, and useful.

## Do Not

* Do not write final polished website copy unless explicitly asked.
* Do not invent business facts.
* Do not assume current website content is accurate if it appears outdated.
* Do not over-focus on SEO at the expense of conversion.
* Do not reuse third-party review content without flagging attribution/approval needs.
* Do not make every service seem equally important.
* Do not bury priority services.
* Do not ignore conflicts between the intake and website.
* Do not turn the output into a generic website audit.
* Do not critique design quality unless asked. Focus on content extraction and planning.
